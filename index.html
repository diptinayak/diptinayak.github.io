I am a Life Sciences Research Foundation Postdoctoral Fellow, and a Carl Woese Institute for Genomic Biology Fellow at the University of Illinois.

My research falls at the intersection of microbial physiology and evolution: I employ tools from both domains to understand the fundamental biology of Archaea, an important class of organisms comprising the third domain of Life, with wide-ranging implications in climate change, renewable fuels, and the carbon cycle.

Biographical Sketch

Dipti is a Life Science Research Foundation Fellow courtesy of the Simon's Foundation (awarded nationally to ~30 from over 1000 candidates in all fields of life sciences), and a Carl Woese Institute for Genomic Biology Fellow at the University of Illinois, working with Prof. William Metcalf. Dipti received her PhD from the Organismic and Evolutionary Biology Department at Harvard University, under the direction of Prof. Chris Marx in 2014, and a Masters degree from the Civil and Environmental Engineering Department at Stanford University in 2009. Her dissertation focused on using experimental evolution to study metabolism and stress response in microorganisms that grow on reduced single-carbon compounds (methylotrophs). Her postdoctoral research has focused on the physiology and evolution of methanogenic archaea, employing CRISPR-Cas9-based genome-editing tools — the first successful application of CRISPR-Cas on a member of the archeal domain.

Dipti received the Best Poster Award (1 out of ~100 posters) at the Gordon Research Conference on Applied and Environmental Microbiology (2017) and the Best Poster Award (3 out of ~100 posters) at the Gordon Research Conference on the Molecular Basis of Microbial One-Carbon Metabolism (2016). She received a Distinction in Teaching from Harvard University (2013), a Young Investigator Oral Abstract Presentation from the American Society of Microbiology (2013), the Sigma Xi Grants in Aid-of-Research (2012), as well as multiple graduate fellowships from Harvard University (2009) and Stanford University (2007). Dipti served as the Chair (2016) and the Vice-Chair (2014) of the Gordon Research Seminar on the Microbial Basis of One-Carbon Metabolism.

Motivation and Research Interests

Methane is a tremendous societal burden and a resource: on the one hand, it is a potent greenhouse gas, and on the other, it is a cheap, renewable fuel. Technological advances are needed at both ends: for mitigating atmospheric emissions, as well as converting organic-rich effluents to methane. At the heart of these problems, and their solutions, lie methanogenic and methanotrophic archaea: the primary producers and consumers of methane respectively.

Despite being ubiquitous, and encompassing a domain of Life distinct from eukaryotes and bacteria, archaea are among the least-characterized life forms on Earth. A strictly anaerobic lifestyle and slow growth have particularly impeded comprehensive studies of methanogenic and methanotrophic archaea; therefore our understanding of these pivotal microorganisms is severely deficient. By integrating an evolutionary framework (from my graduate research) with a rapid, multiplexed genome editing toolkit (developed during my postdoctoral research), I overcome these roadblocks to design a wide-range of experimental platforms to test prevailing hypotheses, as well as discover novel and unique features of methanogenic and methanotrophic archaea.

Broadly, my research is at the intersection of physiology and evolution, with a goal to understand the fundamental and distinctive biology of methanogenic and methanotrophic archaea from an organismal and community perspective. This knowlege is key to designing optimized solutions for aforementioned environmental and biotechnological problems.

Service

I have been privileged to serve the community in various was as a PhD student and PostDoc.

   Chair and Vice-Chair of Gordon Research Seminars

I served as the Vice-Chair (in 2014) and Chair (in 2016) for the Gordon Research Seminar (GRS) on the ‘Molecular Basis of Microbial One-Carbon Metabolism’. This seminar is held for two days prior to the corresponding Gordon Research Conference (GRC) and is organized by and for early-career scientists. As the Chair, my duties involved screening and selecting ~50 participants from all over the globe, inviting prominent scientists as keynote speakers and discussion leaders, and also acquiring external funding for the meeting. In addition, I developed a mentorship component for the 2016 meeting titled ‘Scientific careers beyond the PhD and postdoc’, which included scientists from industry and academia as well as a senior editor for Science. The 2016 meeting spanned twelve talks over two days, along with a keynote.

   Microbial Science Initiative Journal Club

When at Harvard, I helped establish the Microbial Sciences Initiative (MSI) summer journal club, and was in-charge of running this cross-disciplinary event for its first two years. The MSI journal club is now in its sixth year, and continues to attract a number of graduate students and postdocs across campus.

Outreach

I have actively sought out a number of outreach activities as a PhD student and PostDoc.

The Microbiology of Food and Drink: Public Talk and Other Activities

I have designed a broadly accessible talk on the microbiology of food and drink, and delivered it to a number of venues, including the Chambana Science Cafe in Urbana, the Beacon Hill Seminars, and the Science in the News Lecture Series from the Harvard Medical School. I used this talk as a vehicle to introduce some of my work on microbial evolution and physiology. Please email me if you'd like to use my slides! I also collaborated with a cheese monger with a cheese monger to culture and showcase the fascinating microbiology of cheese at the Cambridge Science Festival. I also presented a talk titled ‘The role of hops and the mechanism of bacterial resistance to hops’ at an MSI (Microbial Science Initiative) workshop.

   Interactive Activity for Kids in Grades K-4

I designed an activity for the Institute of Genomic Biology Genome Day highlighting the extremophilic nature of archaea, targeted at kids in grades K-4. The activity showcased archaea as superheroes or supervillains, with the goal of the activity to match the superhero/villian with its extremophilic "superpower". If you're a science teacher and you'd like to use this activity in your class, I would love to speak to you — please reach out!
